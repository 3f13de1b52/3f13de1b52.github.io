An Ode to Pomodoro

20 Aug 2017 by Ozan Gulle

About two months ago, I re-discovered and started applying to pomodoro technique both in my professional and private life. My first encounter with the tomato was back when I was a mere student. It didn’t help me then, because I couldn’t understand a fundamental step in making it work. Now, after about 10 years since we’ve first met, I suddenly understood what it was all about and how to use it.

The technique itself is fairly simple. A pomodoro is a unit of work you do in 25 minutes. You set a timer for 25 minutes and start working. When your timer rings (or beeps or chimes or whatever your timer does) you take a break of 5 minutes. It’s not only important to work for the whole 25 minutes but also to take the break for the whole 5 minutes. You repeat this process three more times ( four times in total) and you’ve earned the right for a longer break from 15 to 30 minutes. I had already understood this part when I was a student. What I failed to grasp was how crucial it is to the whole process to split your tasks into actionable and verifiable tasks. Actionable means you can start working on them immediately and verifiable means you know when they’re done. If you don’t do this last part, pomodoro cannot help you.

When I look at it now, I realize how naive I was miss what at the core of “work” lies. Scott Hanselman explains it nicely in his speech that there are 3 kinds of “work”. You have:

  1. Pre-defined work
  2. Work as it Appears
    3. , and
  3. Defining Works

The second one is the most dangerous one according to Scott Hanselmann (and I agree), because it hinders you ability to plan. When you’re dealing with work as it appears, you’re simply dealing with problems without any plan or direction. The pomodoro technique lets you concentrate on the first kind of work, namely pre-defined work. You should never sit down to a pomodoro session and expect work to appear. You setting that timer presupposes* that you already know what you’re going to do next. But that’s not enough. In order to do that, you must have gone through the third step first (I know, it should have been the first item on the list). You should have already sat down and “worked” on what you are going to work on. This meta-work sounds like an ingenous idea to waste massive amounts of time but it is actually very, very important.

Coming back to the pomodoro technique itself, it’s amazing how such a simple principle can enhance your productivity. It changed the way I work in several ways, but I want to list the two major lessons I learned from this technique.

1. It thought me how much you can do in 25 minutes

I was obsessed with the idea that you need at least an hour to start any work that’s really worth doing. If someone told me to start with something in half an hour, I would have told them that there’s not enough time to do anything.

I was completely wrong. You can do a lot in 25 minutes.

2. It thought me how much it takes to do certain tasks

Since you’re always working with a running timer, you end up having a pretty good idea about how much it takes you to do certain tasks, for example like writing blogposts. It is a considerable boost in self-confidence to be able to successfully predict how much time something is going to cost you.
